How to Establish Visitation in Texas with Your Children

Posted: July 27, 2017

When parents separate, it becomes necessary to determine how the parents will co-parent. Developing a parenting plan that establishes a visitation schedule by the non-custodial parent is part of that plan. Understanding how visitation works in Texas can help you maintain a strong relationship with your children even when you do not live with them full-time.

Understanding Visitation in Texas

In Texas, visitation is legally referred to as "possession and access." This term describes the time that the non-primary parent spends with the child. While one parent typically has the right to determine the child's primary residence, both parents usually have rights to spend time with their children.

The Standard Possession Order

Texas has established a Standard Possession Order (SPO) that serves as the default visitation schedule when parents cannot agree on their own arrangement. The SPO is designed to provide consistent, meaningful time between children and the non-custodial parent.

Under the Standard Possession Order, the non-custodial parent typically receives:

  • First, third, and fifth weekends: From Friday at 6:00 p.m. to Sunday at 6:00 p.m.
  • Thursday evenings: During the school year, from 6:00 p.m. to 8:00 p.m.
  • Alternating holidays: Including Thanksgiving, Christmas, and Spring Break
  • Extended summer possession: 30 days during the summer (or 42 days with the extended SPO)
  • Father's Day or Mother's Day: Depending on which parent it applies to

Expanded Standard Possession Order

Parents can elect an Expanded Standard Possession Order, which provides additional time. Under this arrangement:

  • Weekend possession begins when school ends on Friday (instead of 6:00 p.m.)
  • Weekend possession ends when school begins on Monday morning (instead of Sunday at 6:00 p.m.)
  • Thursday possession during the school year extends overnight until school begins Friday morning

How to Establish Visitation

There are several ways to establish a visitation schedule in Texas:

  1. Agreement between parents: Parents can create their own parenting plan and visitation schedule. This allows for flexibility and can be tailored to the family's specific needs.
  2. Mediation: If parents cannot agree, a mediator can help them reach a compromise. Mediation is often required before going to court.
  3. Court order: If parents cannot reach an agreement, the court will establish a visitation schedule based on the best interests of the child.

Factors the Court Considers

When establishing visitation, the court considers various factors to determine what arrangement serves the child's best interests:

  • The age and developmental needs of the child
  • The work schedules of both parents
  • The distance between the parents' homes
  • The child's school and activity schedules
  • Each parent's ability to care for the child
  • The child's preference (if the child is 12 or older)
  • Any history of family violence or abuse

Special Circumstances

Some situations may require modifications to the standard visitation arrangement:

  • Long-distance situations: When parents live more than 100 miles apart, special provisions apply that modify the standard schedule.
  • Young children: For very young children, courts may order shorter, more frequent visits to maintain bonding with both parents.
  • Safety concerns: If there are concerns about a child's safety, supervised visitation may be ordered.

Enforcing Visitation Rights

If the other parent is not following the court-ordered visitation schedule, you have legal options. You can file a motion for enforcement with the court, which may result in make-up time, fines, or other consequences for the non-compliant parent.

How America Family Law Center Can Help

Establishing visitation is an important step in maintaining your relationship with your children after separation. America Family Law Center can help you understand your rights, negotiate a fair visitation schedule, and take legal action if your visitation rights are being denied.

Whether you are establishing visitation for the first time, seeking to modify an existing order, or need help enforcing your visitation rights, we are here to assist you.
