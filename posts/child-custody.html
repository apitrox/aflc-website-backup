Child Custody

Posted: February 1, 2022

Child Custody can be very confusing with all the terms that are used in television shows. Child Custody terms can even vary from state to state.

Understanding Your Custody Rights

When it comes to child custody, many parents find themselves overwhelmed by the terminology used in family courts. Terms like 50/50 custody, joint custody, sole custody, and full custody are often used interchangeably on television shows and in casual conversation, but they can have very different legal meanings depending on where you live.

In Texas, the term "custody" is rarely used in legal proceedings. Instead, Texas family law uses the term "conservatorship" to describe the legal relationship between a parent and child. Understanding this distinction is crucial when navigating the Texas family court system.

Common Custody Terms Explained

Joint Custody (Joint Managing Conservatorship in Texas): This is the most common arrangement in Texas. Both parents share the rights and duties of raising their child, though this does not necessarily mean equal time with the child. The court typically designates one parent as the primary conservator who has the right to determine the child's primary residence.

Sole Custody (Sole Managing Conservatorship in Texas): In this arrangement, one parent has the exclusive right to make decisions about the child's upbringing. This is typically ordered when one parent is deemed unfit or when there is a history of family violence, abuse, or neglect.

50/50 Custody: While many parents hope for equal time with their children, true 50/50 custody arrangements are less common than you might think. In Texas, the standard possession order typically gives the non-primary parent about 42% of the time with the child. However, parents can agree to expanded or equal possession schedules.

Full Custody: This term is often misunderstood. Many people use it to mean complete control over the child, but in reality, even when one parent has sole managing conservatorship, the other parent typically still has visitation rights and certain legal protections.

Factors Texas Courts Consider

When determining conservatorship in Texas, courts consider several factors, always with the best interest of the child as the primary concern:

  • The physical and emotional needs of the child
  • The emotional and physical danger to the child
  • The parenting abilities of each parent
  • The stability of each parent's home
  • Any history of family violence or substance abuse
  • The child's preferences (if the child is 12 or older)
  • Each parent's willingness to encourage a relationship with the other parent

Finding the Right Child Custody Lawyers

Navigating a child custody case without proper legal representation can be challenging and risky. The right attorney can make a significant difference in the outcome of your case and help protect your parental rights.

Finding Affordable Attorneys

Legal representation in family law matters can be expensive, but there are options for those who cannot afford traditional legal fees:

  • Payment Plans: Many family law attorneys offer payment plans that allow you to spread the cost of representation over time.
  • Limited Scope Representation: Some attorneys offer unbundled legal services where they handle only specific parts of your case, reducing overall costs.
  • Legal Aid Organizations: Organizations like America Family Law Center provide assistance to those who qualify based on income.
  • Law School Clinics: Many law schools operate family law clinics where supervised law students provide free or low-cost legal assistance.

Fathers Rights Lawyers

Fathers facing custody disputes often feel that the legal system is stacked against them. While Texas law does not favor one parent over the other based on gender, fathers may benefit from working with attorneys who have experience advocating for fathers' rights. These attorneys understand the unique challenges fathers face and can help ensure that fathers receive fair treatment in custody proceedings.

Key rights that fathers should be aware of include:

  • The right to be considered equally as a potential primary conservator
  • The right to seek expanded or equal possession time
  • The right to participate in major decisions about the child's education, health care, and religious upbringing
  • The right to access the child's medical and school records

Pro Bono Options

For those who truly cannot afford legal representation, pro bono (free) legal services may be available:

  • Pro Bono Attorneys: Many private attorneys volunteer their time to help low-income individuals with family law matters.
  • Legal Aid Societies: These organizations provide free legal services to qualifying individuals based on income and other factors.
  • Bar Association Referral Programs: Local and state bar associations often maintain lists of attorneys willing to provide pro bono services.
  • Nonprofit Organizations: Organizations like America Family Law Center connect families with legal resources and assistance.
