Determining a Child's Parent Through Adjudicate Parentage

Posted: February 7, 2017

Usually, it is clear who a child's parents are - but not always. Sometimes, it takes a court to make the final determination of the identity of one of a child's parents.

What is Adjudication of Parentage?

Adjudication of parentage is the legal process by which a court determines who a child's legal parents are. This process is most commonly used to establish paternity - identifying who the legal father of a child is - but it can also be used to establish maternity in certain circumstances.

In Texas, establishing parentage is governed by the Texas Family Code, specifically Chapter 160, which is based on the Uniform Parentage Act.

When is Adjudication of Parentage Necessary?

There are several situations where adjudication of parentage may be necessary:

  • When a child is born to unmarried parents and the father has not signed an Acknowledgment of Paternity
  • When there is a dispute about who the biological father is
  • When a man wants to establish his legal rights as a father
  • When a mother wants to establish child support obligations
  • When the state seeks to establish paternity for child support enforcement purposes
  • When a man denies being the father of a child

The Role of DNA Testing

DNA paternity testing plays a crucial role in adjudication of parentage cases. Modern DNA testing is highly accurate, with results that can establish paternity with greater than 99% probability.

In Texas, the court may order genetic testing at the request of any party, or on its own motion if there is a dispute about parentage. The testing typically involves collecting DNA samples from the child, the mother, and the alleged father through a simple cheek swab.

If the genetic testing shows that a man is the biological father, the court will typically enter an order adjudicating him as the legal father. If the testing excludes a man as the biological father, the court will dismiss the paternity action against him.

Who Can File a Parentage Action?

Under Texas law, several parties may file a suit to adjudicate parentage:

  • The child
  • The mother of the child
  • A man whose paternity is to be adjudicated
  • A support enforcement agency
  • An authorized adoption agency
  • A representative of a deceased individual who could have filed a suit

The Legal Process

The process for adjudicating parentage in Texas typically involves the following steps:

  1. Filing a Petition: The process begins when a party files a petition with the court to establish parentage.
  2. Service of Process: The alleged father must be served with notice of the lawsuit.
  3. Response: The alleged father has the opportunity to respond to the petition.
  4. Genetic Testing: The court may order DNA testing if paternity is disputed.
  5. Hearing: A hearing is held where the court considers the evidence, including any genetic test results.
  6. Order: The court enters an order adjudicating parentage, which establishes the legal parent-child relationship.

Effects of Establishing Parentage

Once parentage is established through adjudication, it has significant legal effects:

  • The father gains legal rights to custody and visitation
  • The father becomes legally obligated to pay child support
  • The child gains inheritance rights from the father
  • The child may be eligible for benefits such as Social Security, veterans benefits, or health insurance through the father
  • The child's birth certificate may be amended to include the father's name

Time Limits for Filing

In Texas, there are time limits for filing a parentage action. Generally, a proceeding to adjudicate parentage may be commenced at any time, even after the child reaches adulthood. However, there are some exceptions and limitations that may apply depending on the circumstances of the case.

We understand the emotional and legal complexities involved in parentage cases and are committed to helping you protect your rights and the best interests of the child.
